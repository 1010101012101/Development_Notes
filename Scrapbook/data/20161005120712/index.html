Why Rwanda Must Address the Skills Shortage

7 months ago
by BERNA NAMATA
Add Comment
465 Views
Written by BERNA NAMATA
Rwanda faces the uphill task of addressing the critical shortage of manpower with the skills that are urgently required to reduce poverty and facilitate the transformation of the economy.

The government’s blueprint Vision 2020 emphasizes transforming the economy from agriculturally based to knowledge-based. Specifically, it seeks to move the economy from low productivity-based agriculture to a more industrial and diversified economy.

The government is also targeting creating 200,000 off-farm jobs according to the second Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS2). Recent progress has led to a steady increase in the contribution of the service sector that contributed 48% of gross domestic product (GDP), while the agriculture sector contributed 31% in the first quarter of 2015.

But continuing this progress requires developing a workforce with relevant skills.

 Economic growth and the skills shortage

Rwanda’s economy grew by 7.6% in the first quarter of 2015, up from 7% in the same quarter of 2014, figures from the National Institute of Statistics show. But the country continues to have a huge skills deficit.  The majority of Rwandans remain employed in low paying jobs due to limited skills. In 2012, a survey on 8 key priority sectors revealed an average skills gap at 40% and at 89% for artisans and technicians, government figures show.

In 2011, about 28.5% of modern firms in Rwanda identified inadequate skilled workforce as major constraint, compared to an average of 14.7% in the East Africa Community (EAC).

“As a company established in Rwanda that is trying to expand internationally, we need to be able to attract and retain highly qualified employees. Ideally these would be Rwandans who can work at an internationally competitive level. But, unfortunately, this is not always the case,” observes Íñigo Verduzco, a project manager at Laterite Ltd specialising in program evaluation, econometrics, and policy analysis.

Moreover, the majority of agricultural workers have no educational qualifications and adult illiteracy remains an issue. In fact, up to 68.3% of the labour force have never attended school or have attended but have no qualification, which is a major contributor to youth under-employment and low labour productivity in the informal sector.

The Vision 2020 aims to have 50% of the workforce in off-farm jobs by 2020, up from 28.4% in 2011.  However, while the government has sought to address the skills shortage by opening up the economy to expatriates from abroad and the East African region, experts say the country has to generate a critical mass of local skilled labour to facilitate investment and sustain growth.

“Judging by the unemployment rate among graduates (13.5%), there does seem to be quite a notable mismatch of skills here. But this is a common problem in many countries across the world. OECD research has shown, for instance, that introducing policies to reduce the skills mismatch is one of the fastest ways of improving productivity growth,” says Andrew Mold, a Senior Economic Affairs officer at United Nations Economic Commission Sub-regional office for Eastern Africa UNECA.

To address the skills gap, Mr Mold underscores the need to put more emphasis on TVET rather than university education; revising the kind of courses available at university, and offering more grants/subsidies in subjects where the skill shortages are greatest such as engineering and medical sciences.

“I think the migration of qualified workers creates particular difficulties for developing countries in terms of retaining trained staff required in some sectors (the well-known ‘brain drain’ phenomenon) – and that can only be resolved by persuading the industrialised countries to change their policies towards migration of skilled workers (which is infinitely more accommodating than for unskilled workers). There are, for instance, more Ethiopian doctors working in the US than in Ethiopia,” Mr Mold observes.

The 2013 business and investment climate survey showed that graduates lack basic skills needed for jobs, due to lack of linkages between the education system and industry. For example, in manufacturing, tourism, and ICT, graduates often lack practical experience, and cannot get a job as a result.

Indeed, the education sector faces the challenge of keeping pace with changing needs and the demands of the private sector in order to provide meaningful employment to young graduates. As mentioned previously, the 2012 skills survey revealed a skills gap averaging 40% of the total labour force, especially in TVET.

Foreign expertise?

Still, Rwanda has tried to bridge the shortfall in skilled labour by relaxing visa restrictions and opening its borders to skilled labour not only from the region but also beyond including Europe and America.

President Paul Kagame has said Rwanda’s decision to open its borders to all African nationals was driven by a sense of enlightened self-interest: “Common sense and enlightened self-interest are the reasons we opened our borders to all African nations,” Mr Kagame told World Economic Forum on Africa in Abuja, Nigeria in May 2014.

Since January 2013, African nationals coming to Rwanda are given visas at points of entry. And according to the African Development Bank, Rwanda and Mauritius are top performers in its forthcoming first Africa Visa Openness Index. The Index ranks African countries on the level of openness/restrictiveness of their visa regimes. Its aim is to drive visa policy reforms across Africa, simplify visa application procedures, and encourage positive reciprocity. The Africa Visa Openness Index and documentary will be launched at the African Union Summit in January 2016.

A weak education system

While the government has sought to address the skills gap by stepping up investments in education including technical and vocational schools, quality of education remains a challenge. And while thousands of students are churned out of local universities, a key concern for employers is that the majority of the fresh graduates are half-baked.

Experts underscore the need for Rwanda to couple the science and technology innovation policy with the science sector if the country is to achieve its target of being a knowledge-based economy. This is because the higher you are in knowledge intensity of your population, the higher you are in the value chain gain. However the current skills gap continues to undermine such efforts.

But Rwanda is not alone. According to a recent report by the Inter-University Council for East Africa (IUCEA), a body created to regulate higher education in the regional bloc, most of the universities in the East African Community (EAC) lack employability skills, technical mastery, and basic work-related capabilities. The situation, the report that polled employers says, denies all the five countries making up the EAC the skills needed to drive growth. The study showed that Uganda has the worst record with at least 63% of graduates found to lack job market skills, followed closely by Tanzania, where 61% of graduates were found to be ill prepared.

In Burundi and Rwanda, 55% and 52% of graduates, respectively, were perceived to be incompetent, while in Kenya, 51% were believed to be unfit for jobs. This means that, despite thousands of young people graduating each year, their qualifications are unable to secure many of them jobs.

Experts also point out that many off-farm jobs require employability enhancing skills or prior experience in order to be productive and employable.

Therefore local level initiatives to increase access to skills and training will help equip rural people to acquire relevant skills and better business management tools, including access to finance.
