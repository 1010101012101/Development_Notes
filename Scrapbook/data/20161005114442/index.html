By Moses K Gahigi

Rwandan engineers have complained after being sidelined on many of the ongoing construction projects, saying government has not regulated the market well even though it owns some of the projects.

Kigali has a robust construction sector which is giving a bullish outlook to the economy, but money earned on these projects seems to be exiting the economy, since majority of the technicians are foreigners.

This goes for both government-owned projects, as well as private construction projects.

"The trend is that when a foreign company comes into the market, they bring their professionals, instead of hiring local engineers, and government has let this to happen," said Eng Fred Rwihunda, the president of the Engineers Association in Rwanda.

Mid last year, the government contracted Turkish company Summa Construction Inc. to complete the Kigali Convention Centre however the company flew in engineers from Turkey, who are the ones almost exclusively working on the project.

"The Convention Centre is supposed to be an opportunity for local engineers, but it's the Turks flown in that are doing the technical work. The situation is the same to many projects such as the Marriot Hotel, Vision City and others that have massively employed Chinese workforce," said Mr Rwihunda.

Sources close to the project said that one of the conditions advanced by Summa in the contract, was to bring its engineers and other technicians to work on the project, a condition the government agreed to.

"Some of them are not even registered with us, in case of any fault on the construction after these people have gone back, it is the local engineers who are here to fix. It is therefore important for local engineers to work alongside foreign ones, there are many benefits, but this is also not happening on these big projects," he said.

The Minister of Labour Judith Uwizeye, said her ministry does control the foreign skill that enters the market. "If foreign engineers appear on our market it is because we have a skills gap in that area, we do research every two years and come up with an occupation demand list, which guides us on the skill gaps," she said.

According to the Rwanda Labour information system, produced by Ministry of Public Service and Labour with the help of the International Labour Organisation (ILO), foreigners tend to dominate in key occupations in the construction sector as the country has few local scientists, technicians and even artisans.

In particular, the experts found out that the construction industry lacks qualified Rwandan civil, mechanical, electronic and electrical engineers, building construction artisans (labourers).

Mr Rwihunda did not agree with this. "We have many engineers who can work on these projects, we have engineers who have worked on really big projects." The majority of Rwandans working on these projects are doing casual jobs.

"The people who do the technical works on these project are the Turks, we come in to do the casual jobs, however some of the Turks also do the non-technical work," said Emmy Harerimana, a casual labourer.

He said some of the work the Turkish do on the site is the usual work other Rwandans do on other sites.

Eng John Karamagi, a private engineer in Kigali, said some of the companies that bypass local engineers and hire foreign engineers give reasons like they are beating tight deadlines, with no time to train local engineers.

This has also raised the issue of competent local real estate management companies which bid for local projects and lose only to hear that foreign financiers of the local projects dictated who should be given the contract, and this is always a foreign firm.

Desperation to complete the much delayed Convention Centre must have played into it bypassing local engineers.

Valued at more than $300 million (about Rwf225 billion), the complex which occupies 13.6 hectares, will comprise a five-star hotel with 292 rooms, a conference hall with the capacity to host 2,600 people, several meeting rooms, a museum as well as offices and shopping space.

The project is overseen by the Ultimate Concepts Ltd, a joint venture by the government of Rwanda through the Rwanda Social Security Board and Prime Holdings Ltd, as well as private investors including Crystal Ventures Ltd and Rwanda Investment Group.
